Well that went fast.

It's hard to believe Berkeleyside turned 15 this year.

What began as a scrappy blog and passion project, whose work was conducted around a Berkeley kitchen table, has grown into a seven-journalist strong newsroom that reports on all aspects of Berkeley.

Founders (From L to R) Lance Knobel, Frances Dinkelspiel, Tracey Taylor. Credit: Tippett Studio

The Berkeleyside team in February 2023, clockwise left to right: Nico Savidge, Supriya Yelimeli, Pamela Turntine, Iris Kwok, Alejandra Armstrong, Zac Farber, Ally Markovich. Credit: Tracey Taylor

And we like to think it's still scrappy.

In many ways, Berkeley makes it easy to report on. It is a small city that leaves an outsize mark in the national, even international, imagination. Its citizens, drawn here for its bay-side location, its outlook, and its world-class university, have made it a trailblazing place: It was the first city to adopt single-family zoning and the legacy of that decision lives on, and among the first places to adopt a voluntary busing plan to fully integrate its public schools; the first to successfully levy a tax on sugary drinks and to create a dog park and a tool lending library. The list goes on.

But often it's the smaller stories that capture the attention of our team of journalists — and are most appreciated by our readers: the community coming out to celebrate the retirement of a longstanding postal carrier; the enthusiasm for small independent businesses; the kindness shown by neighbors during the pandemic lockdown; a consistent appreciation of the wildlife that surrounds us.

For much of the city's life, the chronicles of Berkeley could be read in a number of local newspapers. But, by 2009, nobody was consistently telling the story of Berkeley any more. That’s why three journalist friends who had met at their children's school launched a news site to fill the void.

People I meet here tell me all the time that they can't imagine life without Berkeleyside, that it is Berkeleyside's journalism that helps them engage with and enjoy their city.

Photographers Kelly Sullivan and Pete Rosos at work for Berkeleyside. Credit: Berkeleyside

Frances Dinkelspiel and Emilie Raguso in 2015. Credit: Berkeleyside

And Berkeleyside now stands as a national model for what successful, sustainable local news can look like. We used what we learned from the first 10 years of Berkeleyside to form the nonprofit Cityside Journalism Initiative, which has birthed two more local news outlets: The Oaklandside and Richmondside. By sharing central resources and leadership, all of our newsrooms, including Berkeleyside, are significantly strengthened thanks to Cityside.

As we mark Berkeleyside's 15th birthday, the Berkeleyside team and its three co-founders — myself, Frances Dinkelspiel and Lance Knobel — would like to thank the Berkeley community for its support and encouragement on this sometimes bumpy journey. The business of news is hard to crack, but if it was going to work anywhere, it was going to be Berkeley.

To mark the moment, enjoy these 15 Berkeleyside stories that represent the range and depth of 15 years of independent, award-winning local journalism.

Death of a mountain lion in urban Berkeley

The story that put Berkeleyside on the map: A cougar was shot and killed by emergency responders after it was seen wandering around the North Berkeley neighborhood then known as the Gourmet Ghetto in the early hours of Aug. 30, 2010.

Readers asked why the wild cat had not been tranquilized, but a wildlife expert said there had been little choice but to kill the animal.

A disastrous fire on Telegraph Avenue

A five-alarm fire at the Sequoia Apartments at 2441 Haste St. on Nov. 18, 2011 was described as “a hideous development to an already totally beat-up Telegraph” by Marc Weinstein, owner of neighboring Amoeba Music. The disaster, which left dozens homeless but caused no injuries, was a tipping point for Southside, and Berkeley more broadly, in that it underscored the potential perils of older housing stock and ushered in a new era of much needed new housing construction.

Berkeley's Tuolumne Family Camp destroyed, then reborn

Berkeleyans from multiple generations went into mourning after the Tuolumne Family Camp burned to the ground on Sunday, Aug. 25, 2013, a victim of the Rim Fire. Owned and operated by the city, the popular camp along the Tuolumne River near Yosemite National Park had been around since 1922.

The story had a happy ending, however, as nine years later, in May 2022, it reopened. Berkeleyside published a photo gallery of the beautiful new camp that rose from the ashes and welcomed back guests that spring.

4

History made with introduction of soda tax

Berkeley held its most expensive election in 2014 with around $3.6 million spent on two ballot items alone. One of them was the soda tax, a 1-cent-per-ounce tax on sugary drinks. The soda industry spent nearly $2.5 million to defeat it.

Former New York City Mayor Michael Bloomberg donated $657,000 to support it. The measure passed. And it appears to have worked: A 2019 UC Berkeley study showed residents in diverse and low-income Berkeley neighborhoods reported drinking 52% fewer servings of sugary drinks than they did before the soda tax went into law.

5

A balcony collapses with tragic results

Six young lives were lost when a balcony collapsed in downtown Berkeley on the night of June 16, 2015. The tragedy happened when an Irish student who had come to Berkeley to work for the summer, part of a tradition of Irish summer students, was celebrating her 21st birthday with friends in her apartment at what was then called Library Gardens

Thirteen people were on the balcony of the apartment when it sheared off the building. The tragedy was a wake-up call that led to the revelation that more than 400 buildings in Berkeley were in need of repairs, and, eventually, to tougher legislation on balcony construction state-wide.

6

Homeless in one of the most expensive housing markets in the country

Berkeleyside covers the housing crisis from different perspectives, including that of those who have no home or are housing-insecure. In 2016, we joined more than 80 other local media organizations to publish a day of stories, eight in total, focused on homelessness in Berkeley. We spoke with unsheltered individuals, advocates, city staff and service providers. We combed through archives to provide historical context and we described the city’s attempt to address this intractable issue, one that persists to this day.

This series, by Emilie Raguso and Frances Dinkelspiel, won an award from the Society of Professional Journalists, Northern California chapter for explanatory journalism.

7

Berkeley evolves as last big “smokestack” factory shutters

In the fall of 2018, the sprawling Pacific Steel Casting plant with its distinctive blue pipes shut down after 84 years of operating in West Berkeley. The closure of one of the city’s last big “smokestack” factories meant the end of well-paid union jobs, but it also meant neighbors would no longer be concerned that the ‘burnt pot handle’ smell in the air could harm their health. And it symbolized the transition of West Berkeley as a heavy manufacturing hub to a life sciences research and development hub.

8

A radical decision, an unfinished legacy

In 1968, the Berkeley Unified School District voluntarily integrated its elementary schools, taking an unprecedented approach. As has now become well-known, Democratic presidential candidate and Vice President Kamala Harris was bused to Thousand Oaks Elementary School in 1969 as part of the second integrated class there.

On the 50th anniversary of integration in 2018, then Berkeleyside education reporter Natalie Orenstein examined the history and legacy of the move and the equity issues that remain unsolved today.

9

One day, one night: The fuse that lit the Battles of Berkeley

In February 2017, the arrival of the far-right agitator Milo Yiannopoulos on the Cal campus prompted black-clad masked protesters to smash windows and rampage through Berkeley, ultimately causing UC Berkeley to cancel his slated speech. It turned out to be the first of 11 often violent skirmishes, dubbed the Battles of Berkeley, that roiled Berkeley and the university that year.

Our journalists went into the fray over and over to report on the clashes between far-right nationalists intent, they said, on defending their right to free speech and counter protesters acting under the antifa, or anti-fascist, umbrella. We also highlighted the peaceful Aug. 27 demonstration attended by thousands of local residents keen to promote tolerance over hate.

This story won an award from the Society of Professional Journalists, Northern California chapter for best long-form storytelling.

10

Don't call it the Gourmet Ghetto

Little did we realize that an interview with the owner of a new coffee shop in 2019 would start a revolution. When discussing the location of his new cafe, co-owner Nick Cho spoke critically of the moniker long used for North Berkeley’s famed culinary district. Alice Waters followed suit, telling us the neighborhood nickname “has nothing but bad connotations really.” A little more than a month after the story was published, the area’s business district renamed the area North Shattuck.

The article was published by Nosh, the East Bay food vertical that Berkeleyside launched in 2014 and which continues to deliver some of the most popular stories across Cityside’s now three news sites.

11

Berkeley High knew for 15 years of allegations a teacher sexually harassed students

It should probably have come as no surprise that our then education reporter Ally Markovich was awarded a James Madison Freedom of Information Award for her 2021 investigation that revealed how Berkeley High administrators had failed to protect generations of students who accused a chemistry teacher of sexual misconduct. Markovich spent four months interviewing students, researching issues around sexual harm and filing increasingly detailed public records requests seeking the teacher’s disciplinary records. It was only after Berkeleyside threatened a lawsuit that the school district turned over relevant records. In accepting the award, Markovich said it was a testament to Berkeleyside’s willingness to support long-term, in-depth reporting.

12

When COVID-19 pandemic hit, Berkeleyside went to work

When COVID-19 arrived Berkeleyside went into overdrive. We published hundreds of stories, valuable data and useful resources about the coronavirus and how it was affecting Berkeley. We provided the latest medical advice, checked in on how Berkeleyans were coping and pointed readers to vaccination information.

When, in November 2020, we also reported that more than 200 people from the Golden Gate Fields racetrack community had contracted COVID, the presence of nearly 400 people living in rudimentary conditions on the track’s “backstretch” was, for many, as much of a surprise as the news about the contagion.

Four years later, before the 90-year-old track closed for good, we published a special series on the track and explored the uncertain future for those employees, many of them migrant workers from central Mexico, who trained, ran, and took care of the horses.

13

New state housing laws curb power of Berkeley homeowners

In 2010, Berkeleyside’s first full year of publishing, Berkeley approved the construction of just five new homes. In 2022 that number had climbed to nearly 900. And the city has pledged to realize nearly 9,000 new units of housing by 2030. This would have been barely imaginable 15 years ago, when city leaders, under pressure from so-called NIMBY homeowners, put the kibosh on all but a few development proposals.

But with the housing crisis came a power shift as state legislation was enacted that limits local governments’ authority over many development decisions. Married to that came mandates that speed up approvals for affordable housing and backyard cottages, end single-family zoning and allow apartment buildings at BART stations. As a result, Berkeley could look very different a decade from now.

14

People’s Park: A battle for identity meets a need for student housing

The story of People’s Park began long before the arrival of Berkeleyside. Nevertheless, the city’s most fiercely contested land has made news headlines many times over the past 15 years.

In 2024, after decades of clashes between activists and authorities — in the courts, on the grass and in the trees — the university got the green-light to implement its $400 million plan to build housing for students, supportive housing for formerly homeless residents, a park and a historical monument on the 3-acre Southside plot.

In 2023, Berkeleyside’s visual journalist Ximena Natera produced an audio-visual project about the “people of the park” — those who became entwined in the park’s long history and dedicated themselves to stewarding the land, and those who served as a conduit between the university and park advocates.

15

Indian and Mortar Rocks

Despite their impressive size, the boulder outcroppings in North Berkeley can be taken for granted by local residents. In late 2023, Berkeleyside published a richly visual two-part series on Indian and Mortar Rocks.

In words and photos, the articles examined the famous boulders from two perspectives: as places of contemplation, ceremony, gossip and industry for the Ohlone, but also as symbol of a destroyed cultural landscape; and as a place which has taken rock climbing to new heights, attracting, over the years, a fiercely loyal community of climbers who have puzzled over the rock faces and devised new challenges to traverse the rhyolite.
